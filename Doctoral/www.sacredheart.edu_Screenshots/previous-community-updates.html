Dear members of the Sacred Heart University community,

This week we want to talk a little bit about the logistics for reopening and bringing students back to campus.

Over the past few weeks, the University successfully reopened the Tandet Center in Stamford that houses our physician assistant program and the Center for Healthcare Education on Park Avenue that houses the Colleges of Health Professions and Nursing. This was done, following state guidelines, to accommodate those graduate students in the College of Health Professions who needed to finish labs and clinicals that were not completed during the spring semester.

Prior to the return of faculty and students, we prepared the buildings with signage and directional indicators to ensure social distancing. Everyone involved is wearing masks and are adding shields when the lessons necessitate closer quarters. Everyone is taking their temperature before entering classrooms and will stay home if they have a temperature or other symptoms.

We are beginning to narrow down dates for the return of the rest of the student body, and we wanted to share them with you.

  • Early dropoff—Students who want to drop off their belongings into their rooms prior to move-in days will be able to do so by appointment from August 8-18. Information will be coming from residential life about how to make an appointment. This will be for drop-off only. No unpacking should take place until the official move-in days.
  • Freshman Move-in will take place August 25-27. Day and time will be determined by room assignments and will be sent out to freshmen in late July.
  • Upperclass Move-In will be August 28-30 and also based on room assignment. Look for information in late July.
  • Travel Planning—Students who need to make reservations on public transportation, such as planes or trains should choose a date in the correct time frame above and then should notify residential life of the date of arrival on campus. You can make that notification by emailing reslife@sacredheart.edu. Be sure to educate yourself on any quarantining regulations that may be in place here at the time of your arrival.

In all cases, we ask that no more than two family members or friends accompany students for move-in.

Connecticut state guidelines for the opening of universities change according to the rate of infection, so the dates above are subject to change. For example, Connecticut recently announced that visitors from states experiencing a high level of increase in the virus will have to quarantine for 14 days upon arrival. If that is still in effect during move-in, it may impact some of you.

That said, we know that many of you are anxious to make your plans for move-in, so we are sharing our current thoughts on it. You will continue to receive updates on the processes and procedures for move-in through various University communications.

We are not planning to send out a coronavirus communication next week, so we hope that you all have a great holiday week. As you enjoy the holiday and make plans with family and friends, we want to remind you to be careful, wear masks, practice social distancing and wash your hands. The virus is still very much out there, so it is not time to relax our guard. We want all of you to stay safe and healthy.

Thank you,

Dear members of the Sacred Heart University community,

This week’s update focuses on the technology needs of SHU-Flex learning, recommendations for your personal equipment and what SHU is doing to ensure the University is prepared as we continue to navigate our way through this epidemic.

Recommended Equipment

We recommend that all freshman and transfer students bring a laptop computer to allow for participation in and outside of the classroom environment. The University incorporates the Blackboard Learning management system and Echo 360 lecture capture recording, along with a myriad of video collaboration tools and Microsoft Office. For maximum success, your device should be capable of supporting these tools. The laptop you use should have a baseline technical requirement of:

  • Windows 10 or MacOS 10.15
  • 256 SSD GB hard drive,
  • 8gb of ram
  • i5 core processor
  • 13-inch display
  • wireless network connectivity
  • microphone and camera

We also recommended that you have headphones to improve your online web conferencing interactions.

Course work may also utilize Google and Adobe tools, so a scanner app (e.g. Genius Scan) is also recommended. In addition, you should also have multiple internet browsers installed on your computer (Firefox, Chrome for PC users or Safari/Chrome for Mac users). Additional technology resources may be added at the discretion of the course instructor.

Upper-class students who selected a computer from the recommended laptop options during freshman year already have a device capable of meeting the expected technology demands.

Students who use MACs are responsible for ensuring that they are using a newer operating system and that the course instructor can view all submitted assignment files on a PC computer. We do not recommend that you use phones, iPads, Chromebooks, readers or similar tablets as a primary device for completing course assignments.

In addition to a robust and reliable network that allows for anytime, anywhere learning, we also have numerous computer labs for general student use at the Park Avenue Campus, Martire and West Campus as well as a dozen specialized computer labs for specific classes. If you need access to a computer, you may use any of the following spaces when classes are not in session as long as you follow our risk-mitigation measures (mask, social distancing, etc.): HC111, W122, W239, RH-art lab, E1006, E1039, E1129, cyberlab, W109, W114, W118, W133, W141, W146, W208, W213, W217 and W213. 

If you have additional questions related to computer options, please refer to the laptop acquisition information on the website for a detailed description of laptop options supported by our Factory team.

You can also contact our support team at TechSupport@sacredheart.edu. For any questions or general information, contact InfoTech@sacredheart.edu.  

Internet Connections

Internet connections vary across the U.S. and can affect video calls and conferencing. Per FCC regulations, a high-speed broadband connection is determined to have download speeds of 25 Mbps. The higher the number, the better. You can check your speed by visiting https://fast.com/. 

Printing

When you are on campus, you can print documents in room HC113. This room is available to students 24/7. You can also print/scan and copy at any of the existing 25 multifunction printers around campus. Here are instructions on how to print.

Student Success Center

We realize the online components of SHU-Flex will be new for many of you. We want you to know that the staff at the student success center is always available to support your academic success. Be sure to reach out to them any time for assistance with online classes and coursework or for any academic support you may need.   

They are in the process of creating a resource library to help you navigate in the event you find yourself in need of some guidance, clarity or motivation. Dear members of the Sacred Heart University community,

In this week’s message, we will attempt to answer the many questions we have received regarding financial matters. This will include refunds and information about the CARES Act funds and other resources for help for those who need it.

Throughout the COVID-19 period, the University has striven to act fiscally responsibly and fair to our students’ families during this challenging period, while stewarding the University’s resources to ensure we can continue to provide a quality educational experience.

Meal Plans

The University is adjusting meal plans to reflect the reduction in the number of days students will be able to use their fall plan. The adjustment, which varies by plan, will be reflected in your June billing statement. The eliminated Columbus Day four-day weekend and an earlier move-in date will in part offset reduced meals after Thanksgiving, and we have made a prorated reduction for freshman and sophomore plans to reflect the net number of days that students will not be on campus in preparation for any possible virus resurgence. The Red & White meal plans for upper-class students, which are smaller declining balance plans, will have increased fall-to-spring carryover allowances.

Room Charges

Traditionally room charges have covered the length of the semester rather than a daily charge. We will continue our policy of charging a per-semester residence hall rate vs. charging additional fees or refunding payments for one- to two-week differences in access to the halls. Therefore, housing costs will remain as reflected on students’ current bills.

With the health and safety of students as our primary concern, the University is providing additional cleaning and sanitizing of the residence halls, campus classrooms, common spaces, restrooms and shuttles. The University will lease additional beds to allow us to spread students out and for quarantining if needed, and we will considerably expand our health services.

Some of you have commented on what other schools are doing, which is understandable. It’s important to note that the circumstances may differ for each institution. For example, in the spring, we credited/refunded room and meal plan charges to our families and suspended all late fee charges, while other institutions that did not do that then are doing so now.

Tuition

We will not offer a tuition refund as we are continuing to teach for the standard number of hours. Hybrid classes are necessary if we are going to meet the social distancing demanded by the state for us to bring our students back to campus. Our faculty is attending workshops and classes and brainstorming among themselves to ensure the learning experience will be vibrant, challenging, rewarding and fun—both in the classroom and online.

CARES Act

Sacred Heart University is in receipt of its funds from the federal CARES Act: Higher Education Emergency Relief Fund, which provides grants for students who were enrolled last spring for expenses related to the disruption of campus operations due to the COVID-19 crisis. Eligible undergraduate students will receive a $400 emergency grant from this fund. You do not need to take any action at this time. Your relief funding will be sent to your direct deposit refund preference, as defined with Bank Mobile, on Monday, June 8. You can review your preference online at www.refundselection.com. Please allow up to 72 business hours from June 8 for the funding to become available in your bank account.

Please note, this relief funding is not taxable; however, it will be included in box 5 of your 2020 1098T tax form.

Eligible students who are experiencing greater financial distress can apply for additional emergency funds for COVID-19-related expenses. The application and instructions on student eligibility and qualified expenses are now available below.

Sacred Heart Student Emergency Fund

The University has allocated funds to support financial assistance in the form of financial aid and incremental emergency grants to families challenged by this environment. In addition, the Student Emergency Fund, which has been funded by donations from alumni, trustees, faculty and staff, is also available to those who need additional help—especially students who are not eligible for CARES ACT funding. To apply for these funds, use the application on the coronavirus page.

We hope this has answered your questions. We appreciate your patience as we make these decisions and create the processes to best get these relief funds into your hands.

Have a great weekend,

Dear members of the Sacred Heart University community,

The primary focus of this week’s message will be the measures the University is implementing to ensure the health and safety of students, faculty and staff as we prepare to repopulate the campus in preparation for fall classes. We have received a variety of questions about this, and we want to take the opportunity to respond.

Some College of Health Professions graduate students will return to the Center for Healthcare Education in Fairfield and the Tandet Building in Stamford in the next couple weeks to complete clinical labs and other requirements that were delayed by coronavirus restrictions. Those students have been notified of processes and procedures in a separate communication. The University is hard at work securing the necessary supplies and making the adjustments needed to ensure that everyone is as protected as possible when the bulk of our students return in late August.

Initial Return to Campus

All students, faculty and staff will be asked to take their temperature before leaving for campus. Anyone with a temperature of 100.4 or higher will need to remain at home. This will continue to be a daily process before leaving home or a residence hall for campus. Currently state guidelines for reopening require that everyone take a test for COVID-19 upon their return to campus and that residents be tested again 10 to 14 days later. We are working out the details of how that will take place. If someone tests positive, they will be placed in quarantine and contact tracing will take place. 

When arriving on campus, all vehicles will be asked to stop at the welcome booth. Move-in will be spaced over the course of a week, and we are asking that no more than two additional people accompany each student for move-in. We will also be providing an opportunity for students to drop their belongings off in their rooms prior to move-in week. More details about how you can make an appointment for early drop-off will be forthcoming.

Protocol for Residence Halls

Per the state’s reopening guidance, students sharing a room or suite will be considered a “family unit” and will not be required to wear a mask while together within the room. Masks will be required in all common areas such as hallways, lobbies, bathrooms and study/lounge areas. Common areas will be cleaned frequently and thoroughly, and there may be plastic partitions in between sinks in bathrooms. There also may be some restrictions regarding guests in the residence halls. 

Protocol for Campus Common Areas

For the health and safety of everyone, all students, faculty and staff will be required to wear masks whenever they are outside their dorm rooms or offices. Social distancing will be in play with everyone keeping six feet apart in all common areas. Some pedestrian areas may be one-way only. All areas will be clearly marked. There will be more opportunities to use outdoor spaces with additional tables outside of dining halls, such as 63’s and JP’s. In addition, many student activities, club meetings, etc. will take place outdoors. Recent guidance from health officials suggests that we are much safer outside in terms of viral spread. 

Classrooms and Offices

Because of the need to practice social distancing, fewer people will be allowed in classrooms and offices than previously. This means that classes will be hybrid with lessons both in classrooms and online. 

Study Abroad

The University has made the decision to cancel study abroad programs for the fall. We are disappointed that this is necessary because we recognize the importance and breadth of this kind of experience. We hope to accommodate the students who planned to go abroad during an upcoming semester, and we will work to find on-campus living accommodations for students who planned to be abroad and now want to live on campus. 

Pioneer Promise

There will be an ongoing educational campaign to ensure that everyone understands the new protocols and their role in keeping the community healthy. Our slogan will be “Our Wellness at SHU is up to You.” We will ask all students, faculty and staff to sign a document that we are calling the Pioneer Promise. It is a commitment to the new policies to protect us all, but that promise will begin with you. 

CARES Act Funds

Updated information about the distribution of CARES Act funds can be found on the University’s coronavirus page. 

We are sharing what we know as we know it. The policies described above are based on conditions from the state of Connecticut for reopening, along with guidance from local and national health officials and the recommendations from our four task forces. The situation with the coronavirus remains fluid, and it’s possible that some of this will change by the time we start classes. The University task forces are meeting regularly and keeping abreast of any changes and updates from state and health agencies. Still, there is no doubt that things will be different than we have been used to. There will be challenges, but we are a community that rises to the occasion when challenges confront us. We are also a community where closeness and caring are baked into our culture. If we continue to care for and help one another as we always have, we will not only adjust, but we will thrive. 

Thank you—and have a great weekend.

Dear members of the Sacred Heart University community,

Last weekend, we gave you a broad overview of the SHU-Flex model and the University’s plans and protocols for on-campus, face-to-face learning in the fall. Since then, several other universities, such as Notre Dame, Creighton and Rice, have announced similar plans to finish on-ground classes before Thanksgiving. Our plans must be consistent with the guidelines set forth by the State of CT and the CDC, which continue to evolve. Since last week’s message, we have received several questions from you that we will address below. We thank you for your continued patience. In an ideal world, we would not be making any changes at all, but we must adapt our usual way of functioning to maximize the health and safety of the entire SHU community.

As a reminder, the University is planning to begin fall semester classes for undergraduate students as planned on Monday, August 31. However, as part of a plan that we are calling SHU-Flex, we are finishing face-to-face classes for undergraduates early for health and safety reasons. We are eliminating the Columbus Day weekend break and plan to conclude on-campus activities before Thanksgiving break. We are also encouraging graduate courses to complete on-ground activities before Thanksgiving when possible. This plan is designed to mitigate the risk that comes when the majority of the University population travels away from campus and then comes back. We believe this is especially critical since the regular flu season also begins around that time.

Q. Won’t we miss out on some of our education with two fewer weeks in the semester?

A. No. We had stated that we’d planned to conclude the undergraduate semester by the Thanksgiving break, but we are clarifying that. On-ground, face-to-face instruction for undergraduate courses will conclude by Thanksgiving, but the academic calendar will not change. There is now greater flexibility on the part of the instructor to determine exactly how and when each specific course will end, but regardless there will be no face-to-face instruction for undergraduate courses after Thanksgiving, with very rare exceptions.

In cases where the instructor ends the class by 11/24, students would not have fewer instructional hours than they would during a regular 15-week semester. The number of instructional hours will be—and must be—the same as always. This will be achieved by blending in the 4-5 hours (on average) of missed instruction over the course of the 13 weeks of the semester (e.g., extra 20-30 minutes a week of online discussion).

It is also possible that an instructor may choose to hold classes, albeit virtually, following the Thanksgiving holiday. This time may also be used for projects, papers, and/or online final exams. Instructors will make clear to students at the start of the fall term exactly what to expect in the course for the fall so that students may plan accordingly.

Q. I’m concerned that it will be a much more difficult semester when we combine so much into two fewer weeks.

A. Your deans and professors are committed to maximizing your chances of academic success. The student success center will continue to provide various academic resources to support students in their studies. Tutors will be available in all subjects; the Online Writing Lab will run for the entire semester; and the center will provide supplemental instruction in a variety of ways to students—both through group workshops and individual delivery. Supplemental instruction will be available both in person and online.

It’s worth noting that an option for many of you to lighten your course load in the fall is to take a summer or a winter course. The student success center has tripled the number of tutors available to support those taking summer courses. Tutors are available to help students with both course content and general academic success strategies, including how to perform well in an online context. 

At Sacred Heart, we take pride in meeting students where they are and supporting them as they challenge themselves. Whether you decide to study online, take summer courses or follow this year’s fall semester schedule, we have employees throughout the University who are expressly dedicated to student success.

Q. If you want to be done by Thanksgiving, why don’t you just open earlier as other schools are doing?

A. The short answer is that we are unable to do that because it is prohibited by the current CT regulations and guidelines. Regulations vary from state to state depending on the severity and timing of the epidemic, and we must follow the guidelines of our state and local officials. Here in Connecticut, we cannot move students into residence halls before August 24, and we will need the bulk of that week for proper testing and to ensure social distancing during move-in.

Q. If I am nervous about coming back to campus, will I have the option of taking classes online from home?

A. Yes. You should check with your College dean or academic adviser, but most students will have the option to remain at home while taking classes online. The exception might be students who need to be present for clinical courses or labs. If you are planning to stay home, please notify your adviser and also Larry Wielk, dean of students, as soon as possible. His email address is wielkl@sacredheart.edu.  

CARES Act Funds

We have also received quite a few questions about the CARES Act funds. Information can be found further down on the this page. We will update the information there as things change.

The plan that we have laid out for the fall allows the University to adapt to the prevailing health conditions and evolving guidelines of our state and local officials. We hope that this message provides further clarity. We will continue to update you as the various task forces make more decisions and provide more details. We are looking forward to an exciting and engaging semester beginning in just 12 weeks! In the meantime, practice social distancing and wash your hands!

Have a great holiday weekend!

Dear members of the Sacred Heart University community,

This week we want to provide some more information about the University’s planning for the coming semester. There are many details still to be finalized, but we want to give you as much information as we can to help you with your planning. As you might expect, Sacred Heart is following the guidelines recently provided by the governor for the reopening of universities as well as guidance from health experts.

The health and safety of the SHU community has always been our top priority in concert with ensuring the academic continuity that allows our students to complete the requirements needed for their chosen degrees. We recognize that providing an opportunity for students to interact, build friendships, participate in clubs and activities and develop bonds with faculty members is a critical part of the college experience and woven into the fabric of life at Sacred Heart. While there will need to be procedural changes for everyone’s safety, we are determined to provide those experiences to students.

The Fall Semester – Introducing SHU-Flex
Sacred Heart is planning to begin fall semester classes as planned on Monday, August 31. However, as part of a plan that we are calling SHU-Flex, we are condensing the undergraduate academic calendar for safety reasons. We are eliminating the Columbus Day weekend break, but plan to conclude the semester before Thanksgiving break. The plan ensures that students receive the same number of instructional hours as always, with no changes to their already assigned course schedules, but will mitigate risk by limiting trips back and forth to campus that may require additional testing and possible self-isolation. Since most of our graduate students are nonresidents and would present less risk, the graduate calendars will not change, although graduate programs are encouraged to complete on-ground activities before Thanksgiving when possible. This plan is based on the University’s goal to achieve the following:

  • Maintain a safe campus environment through social distancing requirements and other risk-mitigating procedures
  • Maintain teaching excellence whatever the circumstances
  • Comply with all accreditation standards and guidelines
  • Mitigate health risk and minimize travel to home during the semester for residential undergraduate students

To plan for all contingencies, SHU-Flex ensures that the University’s faculty will be prepared to deliver course material both face-to-face and virtually to allow for flexibility for students in various circumstances as well as social distancing requirements. It also enables them to seamlessly switch to alternative modes of teaching at a moment’s notice should a spike in the virus require a change in plans.

In preparation, faculty will participate in development courses and workshops beginning immediately and continuing throughout the summer. We had to pivot quickly this past spring with very little warning, and students and faculty alike did an exceptional job of adapting to the necessary changes. With the time we now have to plan and prepare, we are confident that our hybrid classes will allow for the same close connections with your professors and fellow classmates that we know you value. Our faculty members are excited about the creativity that a hybrid format allows for classes to be more engaging and challenging than the traditional format. And should we need again to move fully online, you will find the experience robust and interactive.

Health and Safety
The wellness task force is also preparing for reopening with the purchase of more than 50,000 masks, along with 30,000 gallons of hand sanitizer, face shields and thermometers, the installation of 80 hand sanitizer pumps at building entrances, signage for social distancing and acquisition of tests. They have also made plans for contact tracing and containment should someone test positive.

In addition, the University is working with its cleaning company, SSC, and carefully planning and implementing comprehensive and frequent cleaning and disinfecting protocols as recommended by the CDC and the American College Health Association for reopening institutions of higher education in the COVID-19 era.

Some additional protocols from the wellness task force that you may see in place include, but are not limited to:

  • Mandatory wearing of masks outside of dorm rooms and offices
  • Extended move-in period
  • Limited guests on campus and between residence halls
  • More takeout and mobile ordering options from dining halls
  • Specified working areas for study groups that are easily sanitized
  • More creative use of indoor and outdoor spaces, such as outdoor liturgies as weather permits
  • Use of video for class guest speakers and colloquia

We realize that some of this information is very broad. The task forces are working hard to develop these plans, and we will continue to share their decisions in these weekly updates.

University Staff
Although the governor has indicated that university business offices can start to reopen beginning Wednesday, May 20, Sacred Heart will not immediately transition staff back to campus. It will be done in a thoughtful, careful way while keeping the safety of everyone in mind. More information about staff transitioning will come from HR by the beginning of June.

Housing and Meal Credits
The student accounts team has completed the process regarding housing and meal credits. Billing statements have been published for the applicable students. Please review your student account information by logging online. Requests for refunds of the meal plan credit may be submitted online at the student accounts website.

Congratulations again to all who received their degrees through the conferral ceremony last weekend. We continue to look forward to the on-ground ceremony that the commencement committee is planning with class of 2020 student leaders.

As we continue to evolve in response to the world we now live in, we are determined to preserve all that makes us the close, caring community we have always been. We are Pioneers, so we will be bold, innovative, creative and resilient. Some things may be different, but we are still the same—immersed in our mission, our traditions, our inclusiveness and our love for one another. See you in the fall.

Dear members of the Sacred Heart University community,

Last week, we shared our hope that the University community will be on campus for the beginning of the fall semester barring any restrictions from health officials and/or the governor. University leadership is proceeding on the assumption that classes will be on-ground and are considering the actions we will need to take to ensure everyone’s health and safety once we are back together.

Our plan to offer a full on-campus program for both residential and commuter students will not come without temporary changes to our typical way of campus life. There will be new procedures and practices that will ensure the wellbeing of our community. There are still many decisions to be made and scenarios to be considered by the task forces we told you about, but some of the things they are considering include:

  • Requirements for testing/lab results for students, faculty, staff and university business partners
  • The use of masks during classes and in public gathering spaces
  • Institution of capacity limits to allow for social distancing in spaces such as workout/exercise facilities and dining halls
  • Modification of programs and events for compliance with limits on crowd sizes and social distancing measures
  • Enhanced sanitizing and cleaning processes

  • Changes to campus access for visitors
  • Enhanced wellness resources to fully support the unique emotional, spiritual and physical wellness needs of our students, faculty and staff

We will continue to update you as decisions are finalized.

We have received some queries about the construction projects on campus. Since the state did not ever shut down construction, the work on our new residence and dining halls and on the parking garage has continued, and those projects are expected to finish on time. As we continue to follow the fluid and rapidly evolving coronavirus crisis, and in light of our move to online learning for the rest of the semester, we are now making the decision to move eligible employees to telecommuting.

Working Remotely

While the university remains open, we are now identifying employees who can work remotely. We need to ensure that the University continues to function, while also taking every precaution to reduce the chance of bringing the virus to campus. Telecommuting will begin tomorrow—Tuesday, March 17—and will continue until further notice.

  • Eligible employees should work from home when possible, provided it does not interfere with university operations (see telecommuting policy).
  • Eligible employees are those whose functions can be performed remotely as determined by their supervisor and approved by their divisional vice president. 
  • Eligible employees should take with them tonight whatever they need to successfully work from home. They should also forward their work phones to their home or cell phones. We expect all phones to continue to be answered in a timely way as we continue to serve our students, parents, alumni and other stakeholders.
  • Eligible employees should determine that they have remote access before planning to telecommute.
  • Supervisors will also assess whether certain employees have extra capacity or time given the reduction in on-campus activity, and whether those employees can perform additional or other duties in support of business continuity. 
  • With the best interests of the University in mind, Sacred Heart may, at any time, ask a telecommuting employee to return to campus to work.  

We are taking these measures not only to protect those employees who will be telecommuting, but also those who will need to remain on campus in jobs that cannot be performed from home. Reducing the population of folks on campus will allow the remaining employees to better practice social distancing and other safety measures.

Staff Who Cannot Work Remotely

While we are encouraging employees to telecommute, some positions (by nature of their responsibilities) cannot work remotely. We are asking those employees to follow the guidelines below to ensure safety and minimize risk associated with the further spread of infection:

  • Absolutely do not come to work if you are feeling sick (see sick leave section below for more information).
  • Implement social distancing practices to reduce close contact in the workplace (e.g. maintain a distance of 6 feet from coworkers).
  • Conduct all meetings by conference call/WebEx.
  • Limit any group discussions or gatherings to no more than four employees.

This Section Only Applies to Essential Employees Who Are Reporting To Work

If you get sick, stay home. Do NOT come to campus.

Your health and the health of our community is our primary concern. Please follow the University's sick policy, and notify your manager if you are sick.

  • If you are concerned that you may have contracted the coronavirus, call your health-care provider to help you to determine whether self-isolation or self-quarantine is needed.
  • If you are told to self-quarantine, even if you are not symptomatic, stay home and notify your supervisor.

Sick Leave

We will ensure that all employees are able to stay at home during the required 14-day period of self-isolation or quarantine.

  • If you have been directed by your health-care provider to self-isolate or self-quarantine because of a coronavirus diagnosis, symptoms or your exposure to a known coronavirus case, you should stay home. You will be provided leave for the mandated self-isolation/self-quarantine period up to 14 calendar days. This means that you will be given up to 10 business days of coronavirus leave, and the time will not impact your accrued sick days or short-term disability.
  • If you use up the 10 business days of coronavirus leave, refer to the sick leave policy.
  • If you are not symptomatic during the period of self-isolation or self-quarantine, and working from home is feasible, you may coordinate with your supervisor to telecommute during that period.

Please reach out to your supervisor, or human resources for guidance about any additional issues or concerns that you have. Please also remember that the University’s EAP program is available to you if you need to talk to someone. The toll-free number (1-800-854-1446) and website are available to you 24 hours a day, 365 days a year.

We are aware that there is a rumor that we have had two nursing students test positive for the coronavirus. That is NOT the case. Two clinical nursing students were in the presence of a patient who is being tested for potential coronavirus. As a precaution, the students did not return to campus and are self-isolating at least until the test results on the patient come in. Should the test be positive, they will remain in isolation for the full 14 days.

Given the decisions of the U.S. government regarding international travel, we have made the decision to have all study abroad students return to their homes where they will continue their studies online. The Office of Global Affairs is providing logistical guidance and support.

Given that the World Health Organization declared the coronavirus a pandemic yesterday, you should be prepared for the continuation of classes that are currently online for the foreseeable future. We will maintain a high-quality learning experience and see that students successfully continue their studies. In particular, we are committed to ensuring that seniors complete the necessary requirements for graduation. We are fully equipped for this. 

The NCAA announced this afternoon that all winter and spring sports are cancelled. The Northeast Conference (NEC) has announced that the remainder of the 2020 NEC Women’s Basketball Tournament and the upcoming NEC Women’s Bowling Tournament are canceled, and all spring sports conference play is suspended. Atlantic Hockey has canceled the remainder of the 2020 tournament, including SHU’s quarterfinal game that was slated for tonight.

In the interest of further facilitating social distancing on campus, Sacred Heart University has made the additional decision to suspend all intercollegiate team and individual competitions until further notice.

The plays scheduled for the next two weekends in the Little Theatre have been cancelled.

Students can choose to return home and pursue their studies online from there, but the campus remains fully operational, and services continue to be available to students. This includes residence halls and dining services for those who choose to stay. Anyone with questions or concerns should not hesitate to contact professors, academic advisers or deans. The counseling center is also available to help with any uncertainty, unease, anxiety or stress. You can contact them at 203-371-7955.

We will continue to update you as things evolve. For additional information and answers to frequently asked questions (FAQs), please visit our coronavirus page on the website.

Thank you for your patience and adaptability as we continue to take proactive measures to prevent and mitigate the risk of a coronavirus spread on campus. You continue to exemplify the very best of SHU, and we appreciate it.

The Coronavirus Planning Team

I want to take a moment to clarify the University’s recent decisions in its effort to mitigate the risk of coronavirus. 

While we still think the risk to our campus is low, we are seeing community spread in neighboring states and across the country. There are currently no known or suspected cases of coronavirus on our campus. We know that we cannot eliminate all risk, but we want to take any steps we can to minimize it. With that in mind and based on the recommendations of many, we decided to be proactive and exercise an abundance of caution now.

To help mitigate the growing risk of transmission, we moved most classes online, postponed or cancelled major events and suspended nonessential travel. For students who choose to stay, we are maintaining all academic support services, residence and dining halls and recreational services. Additionally, all admitted student days, tours and athletic events will continue as planned.

The CDC and local health officials are recommending that we all practice social distancing. Social distancing means avoiding situations where large groups of people are gathered in close proximity for extended lengths of time. This includes classrooms, meeting rooms, event spaces and more. The health and safety of our community members are always our top priority, so to minimize the risk of large gatherings and close quarters, we are using online tools for classes and meetings wherever possible. Where possible and appropriate, we will continue to support educational, student life and athletics activities that require physical presence. That includes some clinical labs and other select courses. While we are doing our best to limit our clinical labs, we also want to ensure that students are able to effectively finish their programs of study in a timely manner. In-person clinicals are necessary for graduation and licensing, and we don’t want to put those in jeopardy if we can avoid it. 

For more information, we have a frequently asked questions (FAQs) document on our coronavirus webpage that we are continually updating. Students with academic concerns can continue to work with our student success center by calling 203-371-7820. If you are experiencing anxiety or excessive stress, you can reach out to the counseling center at 203-371-7995.

These decisions were not made lightly. A signature strength of Sacred Heart is our close, caring community, our day-to-day and face-to-face interactions, our small classes and strong relationships between professors and students. We understand that these measures are inconvenient and disruptive, but we believe they will help minimize the risk to the well-being of our community members and the larger community. We are constantly monitoring the situation and are committed to returning to normalcy as soon as we are able.

As we have said all along, this situation is extremely fluid. We will continue to respond accordingly and update you as new changes are made. The coronavirus planning team has been working hard to make the best decisions based on the information at hand. I ask all of you to support their work and policies, which have been made with the best interests of the SHU community in mind. Again, our strength has always been the way we care for one another. We need that caring and understanding now more than ever.

Thank you,
John J. Petillo

Welcome back from break. In today’s update, we want to tell you about the precautions we have taken—and will continue to take—to protect the University community from the coronavirus. These are precautions recommended by the CDC and local health agencies:

  • Bathrooms in all University housing have been disinfected in anticipation of students’ return
  • Touchpoints (doorknobs, faucets, flushers, etc.) will be disinfected daily with more frequent cleaning and sanitizing of public/common restrooms
  • Extra attention is also being paid to athletic areas because of the high traffic flow
    • Gym equipment is disinfected daily and wiped down with Clorox wipes
    • Hand soap is available in all rest rooms and hand sanitizer can be found throughout the gym area
    • Clorox disinfecting wipes are also available throughout the gym area. These kill 99.9% of all bacteria in 10 seconds
  • In addition, extra protocols will be in place in dining halls
    • Food services personnel will go above and beyond in following CDC hygiene guidelines Hand sanitizers will be available to workers at all food preparation sites
    • Touchpoints (doorknobs, horizontal services, tables & chairs, condiment dispensers, drink dispensers, etc.) will be cleaned often
    • In addition to other locations on campus, portable sanitizer stations will available for students, faculty and staff in the dining halls
    • We ask that you practice good food hygiene—don’t touch food with your hands (a piece of fruit, for example) and then put it back
  • Cleaning staff is available 24/7 to respond to any emergencies anywhere. Monday through Friday from 7 a.m. to 4 p.m., call 203-371-7870. Nights and weekend, students should report issues to their RHD to submit an off-hours MOP request

We are also asking that all students and employees do their part by following the hygiene guidelines set forth by the CDC:

  • Avoid close contact with people who are sick
  • Avoid touching your eyes, nose and mouth
  • Stay home when you are sick
  • Cover your cough or sneeze with a tissue, then throw the tissue in the trash
  • Clean and disinfect frequently touched objects and surfaces using a regular household cleaning spray or wipes. 

We are in regular contact with our cleaning contractors and food services staff to ensure that CDC guidelines are being followed in all areas of the University.

Students

First and foremost, any students who are currently feeling sick should not come back to campus tomorrow. At a minimum, students with fever and respiratory symptom should not come back until they have been fever-free for at least 24 hours without the use of fever-reducing medications.

Students who start to have symptoms of the virus after their return to campus should not walk into Student Health Services. This is because we don’t want to expose others who may be there and is in keeping with CDC guidelines. Instead, please call 203-371-7838 for instructions or to schedule an appointment. A phone history will be taken, and guidance will be given. It is unclear right now if Health Services will be able to test for the virus because of a shortage of available tests.

The flu is still a bigger concern at this time. There was a lot of flu activity on campus just before spring break. It is not too late to get a flu shot, and there are a limited number of flu shots available at Student Health Services for $20.

Faculty & Staff

Faculty and staff members should not be on campus if they are feeling sick.

Faculty members should already be developing an instructional contingency plan just in case on-ground instruction is interrupted. Any faculty member who has symptoms, but is feeling well enough to teach remotely can do so using his or her preferred method of connecting with students.

Staff members who feel sick should stay home and report to their supervisor. It may be possible for some staff who feel up to it to work from home. If you have any questions or concerns about the need to stay home, you should discuss them with your supervisor.

The CDC is advising that you do not walk into a doctor’s office if you think you may have coronavirus so that you do not infect others who are there. Rather, you should call your doctor’s office for guidance. As a reminder, Aetna is providing coronavirus diagnostic testing and telemedicine visits with no out-of-pocket costs or cost sharing for Aetna members.

Over the past few weeks, we have been monitoring the news on the Coronavirus, and I want to update you on some precautions we have taken and talk about what we all should be doing to prepare for a possible outbreak here. As always, the health and safety of our students, faculty, staff and visitors is our top priority.

This week, we made the difficult decisions to cancel the choir’s planned trip to Rome over spring break and also to bring home the 17 students who have been studying at John Cabot University in Rome as part of our study abroad program. We are encouraging those students to limit their travel and use extreme hygiene precautions. As information becomes available, we will make the necessary decisions to keep all members of our community healthy. For now, we are asking anyone returning from an impacted area to self-isolate for 14 days before returning to campus. Anyone who has flu-like symptoms should see a doctor immediately.

As is usually the case with these viruses, the most vulnerable are the elderly. Communities like ours—where people live closely together—are also at risk for the rapid spread of any illness. We already have emergency plans in place and are reviewing and updating them as information becomes available. I am asking all of you to be proactive in preventing an outbreak here. Please follow the hygiene guidelines, especially frequently and thoroughly washing your hands and using sanitizer gel. If you have cold or flu symptoms, please stay home. You should also see a doctor as early as possible.

I want to emphasize that there is no need to panic. We are simply exercising common sense with precautionary measures to keep us all healthy. We will continue to monitor the progress of the virus and will make future decisions based on the guidelines of the CDC and other health agencies.
