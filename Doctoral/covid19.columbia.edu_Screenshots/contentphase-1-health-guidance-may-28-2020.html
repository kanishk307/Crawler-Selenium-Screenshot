Phase 1 Health Guidance - May 28, 2020

Last updated: June 20, 2020

Columbia is committed to providing a safe and healthy environment for all its faculty, staff, students, and visitors. We have therefore developed the following COVID-19 Public Health Guidance in response to the pandemic. Our goal is to minimize the potential for transmission of SARS-CoV-2, the virus that causes COVID-19, in our community. Achieving this goal will require full cooperation among all community members. Each member of the Columbia community is responsible for implementing and adhering with all aspects of the COVID-19 Guidance for their own well-being and for the collective good.

The guidance focuses on the implementation of measures that have been proven to be effective in controlling the spread of the novel coronavirus or where there is evolving encouraging evidence. The SARS-CoV-2 virus is spread primarily by respiratory secretions expelled from the mouth and nose and by touching of contaminated surfaces followed by touching of the mouth, nose, or eyes. Effective preventive measures recognized to date include social/ physical distancing, hand and cough/sneezing hygiene, face covering or personal protective equipment (PPE), as appropriate for role, cleansing of high-touch surfaces, and staying home if sick as per Centers for Disease Control (CDC) guidelines.

Each member of the Columbia community is responsible for implementing and adhering to all aspects of the COVID-19 Guidance for their own well-being and for the collective good.

The information below is intended to apply to individuals on campus currently and those to return on campus as part of Phase 1 as designated in the table below. It is anticipated that Phase 1 for the University will be initiated upon repeal of the “stay-at-home” order by Governor Andrew Cuomo for the New York City region. This document designates “phases” using CDC/OSHA definitions. Specific Phase 1 activities are to be defined by each school, institute, and other entities at the University.

This document will be updated with Phase 2 guidance once Phase 1 requirements are met.

Inform employees and students about proper hand hygiene, cough and sneezing etiquette, and proper use of face coverings/PPE, including instruction or information on:

  • Wash hands often with soap and water for at least 20 seconds or use hand sanitizer with at least 60% alcohol if soap and water are not available. Inform all that if their hands are visibly dirty, they should use soap and water over hand sanitizer. Key times for cleaning hands include:
    • When arriving to the office
    • Before and after work breaks
    • After blowing of nose, coughing, or sneezing
    • After using the restroom
    • Before eating or preparing food
    • After putting on, touching, or removing cloth face coverings
  • Avoid touching eyes, nose, and mouth with unwashed hands.
  • Cover mouth and nose with a tissue when coughing or sneezing, or use the inside of elbow. Throw used tissues into no-touch trash cans and immediately wash hands with soap and water for at least 20 seconds. If soap and water are not available, use hand sanitizer containing at least 60% alcohol (see CDC coughing and sneezing etiquette for further guidance).
  • Provide soap and water in shared working and living spaces (workplace settings, classrooms, residence halls, etc.). If soap and water are not readily available, use alcohol-based hand sanitizer that is at least 60% alcohol. Ensure that adequate supplies are maintained. Increase hand sanitizer availability in public spaces.
  • Provide no-touch trash cans and tissues and place touch-less hand sanitizer stations in multiple locations to encourage hand hygiene.
  • Post signs on how to stop the spread of SARS-CoV-2 virus (i.e., how to properly wash hands and wear a face covering, sneezing and coughing etiquette, etc.) at the entrance of buildings and in other areas where they are likely to be seen. This should include signs for non-English speakers, as needed.
  • Face covering of the mouth and nose is required at all times while on University property, including outdoor spaces. Face covering may be removed by individuals in single offices when no other individuals are present and the door is closed. This is the only exception to the requirement for face covering.
  • Provide bystander training to enable members of the University community to intervene in socially appropriate ways if they observe individuals with lapses in face covering.
  • For shared living spaces operating in Phase 1, face covering is required with the only exception being a single occupancy bedroom.
  • OSHA’s Guidance to Preparing Workplaces for COVID-19 should be used to determine risk level and the appropriate personal protective equipment.
  • Maintain virtual operations for nonessential activities and resuming on-campus activities within the lower risk category based on risk matrix developed by each school/institute/entity
  • For University facilities (offices, labs, classrooms, dormitories, dining halls, libraries, etc.) and transportation shuttles, occupancy levels should be based upon social/ physical distancing guidance and based on type of space, in combination with proper use of face coverings
  • For dormitory and shared-living facilities, efforts should be made to adhere to physical distancing and other measures as articulated in this document.
  • Space seating at least 6 feet apart in offices, classrooms, labs, conference rooms, communal spaces, etc., and turn seats to face in the same direction (rather than facing each other) to reduce transmission caused from virus-containing droplets (e.g., from talking, coughing, sneezing).
  • For office spaces, ensure that one person per room or whenever possible maintain proper distance per person if space is shared. If appropriate distance is not attainable, an adequate partition should be installed and all occupants should wear face covering while in the space.
  • Provide extra time at the start and end of in-person aggregated activities to allow individuals to enter and exit buildings, classrooms, restrooms while maintaining 6 feet of physical distancing.
  • Limit ridership in elevators (based on size) and encourage individuals to take the stairs if able. When utilizing the elevator, all passengers must wear face coverings.
  • Use signs, tape marks, or other visual cues such as decals or colored tape on the floor, placed 6 feet apart, to indicate where to stand when physical barriers are not possible, particularly in building lobbies and communal spaces.
  • Consider staggering employee work hours to avoid congestion in mass transit and entry and exit from buildings.
  • Provide bystander training to enable members of the University community to intervene in socially appropriate ways if they observe lapses in social/ physical distancing.
  • Install plexiglass partitions if social/ physical distancing is not feasible.

The current SARS-CoV-2 testing strategy at Columbia University includes the following:

  •  Individuals with symptoms consistent with COVID-19 and those with history of contact with presumed or confirmed COVID-19 case are strongly encouraged to test for SARS-CoV-2.
  • Individuals who will be returning to campus as of June 22 as part of the research ramp-up phase are required to have initial SARS-CoV-2 testing within 14 days of return to campus (either before or after date of return). These individuals are likely to be returning from other parts of the country, including possibly from communities with active SARS-CoV-2 transmission, and will be re-entering a closed community of individuals who have been on campus.
  • Individuals who have been consistently on campus prior to June 22 are not required to get SARS-CoV-2 testing. These individuals have been in the New York City area, which has experienced a substantial decrease in burden of COVID-19 cases. However, such individuals can obtain SARS-CoV-2 testing, if they desire to do so. 

SARS-CoV-2 testing is confidential. Individuals who test positive are not required to disclose the result of their test.

It should be noted that public health measures such as consistent face covering, physical distancing, hand washing, staying home when sick among others, remain the most important measures for prevention of transmission of SARS-CoV-2.

  • Review is ongoing of testing technologies and capacity, test performance, logistical and practical issues, acceptability and feasibility of routine/mandatory testing and the role of testing for epidemic control to determine utility of follow-on testing or conduct of SARS-CoV-2 testing on sample of employees and/or students.
  • Observe other SARS-CoV-2 testing criteria, consistent with CDC guidelines.
  • Primary contact tracing responsibilities and procedures for identified cases of COVID-19 will not be the primary function of the University, though assistance and support will be provided to the New York City Health and Hospitals regarding contacts within the University, as appropriate. Whether diagnosed by PCR testing at a Columbia University/New York Presbyterian laboratory or at any other laboratory, such laboratories are required to notify the NYC Health & Hospitals of any positive test. New York City is generally responsible for contact tracing and informing individuals who have had contacts that put them at risk of infection, advising them regarding self-quarantine and testing, and providing the necessary support, in addition to maintaining confidentiality. Information regarding contact tracing technologies is evolving and the use of such tools by New York State and New York City in such activities will be taken into account. 
  • Develop, implement, and maintain a plan to perform regular cleanings to reduce the risk of exposure to SARS-CoV-2 consistent with CDC Guidance for Cleaning and Disinfecting.
  • Clean and disinfect surfaces and objects that are frequently touched at least daily. This may include cleaning objects/surfaces not ordinarily cleaned daily (e.g., doorknobs, light switches, elevator buttons, etc.). Use all cleaning products according to the directions on the label. For disinfection most common EPA-registered household disinfectants should be effective.
  • Clean shared workplace and classroom equipment including laboratory equipment, desks, copying/scanning machines, faxes, shared computer keyboards and mouse devices, and telephones, before and after use.
  • A list of products that are EPA-approved for use against SARS-CoV-2 virus  is available here. Following the manufacturer’s instructions for all cleaning and disinfection products (e.g., concentration, application method and contact time, etc.).
  • Discourage individuals from using each other’s phones, desks, offices, or other work tools and equipment, when possible.
  • Provide disposable disinfecting wipes so that individuals can wipe down commonly used surfaces (e.g., doorknobs, keyboards, remote controls, desks, other work tools and equipment) before each use.
  • Secure adequate supplies to support cleaning and disinfection practices.
  • Ensure ventilation systems operate properly and increase circulation of outdoor air as much as possible by opening windows and doors, using fans, or other methods.

Observe University travel restrictions and consult with CDC guidance. 

  • Adhere to safety practices and self-care for successful re-activation of Phase 1 activities at the University and throughout the response and recovery phases from COVID-19 pandemic.
  • Inform supervisor if concerned about own or colleagues’ safety. If supervisor is not responsive, then issue should be raised with their dean, dean of students, Faculty Affairs or Human Resources Department. The University has a policy in place of non-retaliation against individuals who raise concerns based on legitimate motivations.
  • Intervene in non-confrontational way if observe evidence of stigmatizing or discriminatory behavior against individuals who have had COVID-19 or groups perceived to be at risk for SARS-CoV-2, if comfortable doing so.  Stigma can lead to reluctance to disclose symptoms or diagnosis of COVID-19 or prevent individuals from seeking testing and care.
  • Report incidents of stigmatization or discrimination to supervisor or if not responsive to Dean, Dean of Students, Faculty Affairs or Human Resources Department.  
  • Relevant resources are available from the Office of University Life.
