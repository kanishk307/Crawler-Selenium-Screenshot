      Information and Ideas for Final Exams and Grades

      Last Updated: Thursday, April 9th, 2020 at 04:00 PM.
      • COVID-19
      • Campus Communications

      Dear Faculty, 

      Thank you for your work over the past few weeks as you moved your courses to remote format. With two weeks of classes remaining we wanted to offer you some thoughts and ideas to support your work. We wish to remind you that students are not permitted to come to campus for any part of their final exams.

      Reminder from the Registrar: Winter 2020 Final Grades

      As you know with the COVID 19, we have been navigating through choppy waters this semester. In an effort to assist our students with the uncertainty of their performance with the transition to an online teaching format they have been given the option to choose the Pass/Fail grade. Students were notified on March 26 of this option and have had some time to make their decision.

      Faculty will enter letter grades in SIS as usual. After the grade submission deadline of April 29 at 10:00 am, the Registrar’s Office will make the grade conversions for those students who have opted in to Pass/Fail. Because these changes will need to be made manually and will take some time, it is extremely important that all Winter 2020 grades are submitted by the April 29, 2020, 10:00 am deadline. Submitting grades late or making grade changes after this date will delay the following:

      • Final graduation audits 
      • Conferring degrees for April graduates
      • Final transcripts and diplomas for April graduates
      • Minimum grades required for pre-requisites for Spring courses
      • Academic standing decisions
      • Honors designations and Dean’s lists
      • Satisfactory Academic Progress decisions for students receiving financial aid

      Any questions regarding late grades can be directed to the registrar, Karen Arnould (karnould@umich.edu).

      Respondus Test Security

      You have access to both Respondus Lockdown Browser and Respondus Monitor if you would like added exam security. The Lockdown Browser ‘locks’ the computer a student is using such that only the exam is open and nothing else. Students can’t navigate away or open other applications to look up answers. Monitor is a virtual proctoring application that records the student taking the test and then flags suspicious behavior for faculty to review.

      • Faculty: Respondus Lockdown Browser + Monitor
      • Students: Respondus Lockdown Browser

      Workshops: The Office of Extended Learning is offering a series of workshops, including the following, to assist faculty in their exam development and security. See OEL’s workshops page for a full list and to register.

      Delivering Tests in Blackboard

      In this workshop we’ll explore the basics of delivering tests online. Topics will include creating a test in Blackboard, adapting an existing test for online delivery, exam security, and recommended practices for online testing.

      Respondus Lockdown Browser & Respondus Monitor

      This session details the Respondus Lockdown Browser, which is a restricted web browser that you can require students to use when taking your quizzes and exams in Blackboard. When students take an exam using the Lockdown Browser, they will not be allowed to print, copy, go to another website, or access other applications. If desired, you can enable webcam monitoring, which will use facial recognition and other tools to flag test attempts that appear suspicious. The recording for any attempt may then be viewed by the instructor.

      Exam or Final Assessment Options

      Potential Impact on Student Performance and Well-Being

      If you are able to give an alternative assessment as your final exam, consider:

      Low-stakes assignments online could include a reflection on course learning (see list below), such as an essay connecting or synthesizing various topics in the course. These assignments could be submitted and graded in Blackboard.

      An equitable practice; reduces anxiety for students; uses reflection as a good practice for enhancing and demonstrating learning; reduces opportunities for academic dishonesty

      Open-book alternative assessment or assignment online:

      A mostly multiple-choice exam could be reconfigured into a short answer online assessment; a traditional exam could be changed into a graded alternative assignment such as:

      • student video presentations with responses in a Discussion
      • culminating written papers
      • case studies
      • group projects
      • peer-graded assessments
      • or some combination of the above

      Open-book alternative assessments can be more equitable and may be preferred by students during times of distress. Faculty will need to take into account student familiarity with these types of alternative assessments and communicate their expectations clearly.

      Oral Exams:

      The student downloads the exam, develops answers/arguments, and either records or joins a live session where they convey that to the instructor directly. 

      Allows students synchronous time to focus on the exam content and leave other time in their week to focus on other exams.

      Reflection: 

      Providing guiding questions for student reflection results in more robust and thoughtful responses. Here is a list of different types of reflection types and example questions for each type: Reflection Types

      Allows students time to focus on the experience of the course and demonstrate learning through deep critical thinking. This could be a journal assignment or a guided discussion thread.

      If you need to preserve your final exam in its original form, consider:

      Unproctored exam online (open book) using Blackboard quizzes or Assignments.

      Expect some anxiety for students new to online testing, although that tends to be less of a concern for open book exams.

      Open book decreases the summative function of the exam, but this issue may be outweighed by the exam’s potential to promote additional learning. This practice is more equitable than 4, below. Some faculty choose to make unproctored exams timed; in this case, we recommend giving students a full 8-hour day to take their exam.

      Online, proctored exam (closed book) Note: Unless you already have included online proctoring in your syllabus, resorting to online proctoring at this point in the semester (with a site such as ProctorU), is not a good idea.

      Proctored exams are charged directly to students and they tend to increase student anxiety; also, students may lack workspace and/or technology to carry out an uninterrupted, proctored exam. Students may already be experiencing high stress due to public health concerns, and a high stakes exam may exacerbate this, resulting in poor performance.
