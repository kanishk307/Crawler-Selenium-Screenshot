Prepare for Remote Synchronous Learning

What is Remote Synchronous Learning?

Remote synchronous learning begins March 23
Synchronous remote teaching means that instructors and students are in different locations but are nevertheless meeting at the same time in order to interact and engage in â€œreal timeâ€ or â€œnear-real time.â€ This is accomplished through the use of online tools that allow instructors and students, despite their physical locations, to participate in lectures, discussions, and group/individual work. This guide will provide you with some actions and tips for making this shift quickly and effectively.

What to expect as a student:

  1. Expect communication from your professor

Students can expect that their instructors will communicate any changes regarding courses or assignments. Students can expect to hear from their faculty members by March 23 regarding plans for each course.

Students can expect that their instructors will communicate any changes regarding courses or assignments. This may include a revised course syllabus or instructions on how to complete an assignment in a revised way. Faculty will be available to answer student questions and to make themselves for virtual office hours.

  1. Participate in all required course activities, including virtual course meetings

Moving forward, students should expect to participate in all required course activities, including virtual course meetings, following the established course schedule, keeping in mind that they may have to account for time zone changes. Students who have registered with Student Accessibility Services can expect their instructors to continue to provide approved accommodations. Plans are being made to continue student access to support services, such as tutors or the Writing Center, and details are forthcoming regarding how to access these services and hours of operation.

Courses that have experiential learning requirements:
(e.g., labs, arts courses, community involvement, internships, independent studies, etc.)

The shift to synchronous online learning is a significant change for the University, and this shift requires creative thinking on the part of our faculty and our students. Certain class formats, such as studio art courses, lab courses, or internships, may present special challenges to deliver instruction in a remote format. Trinity University has a dedicated center for pedagogical and professional development for faculty called the Collaborative for Learning and Teaching (we call it the "Collaborative" for short). Personnel in the Collaborative will hold workshops and identify resources that will help faculty think through ways to meet core course objectives in new ways, including the development of new assignments or the use of assistive technologies. Students should look to their course instructor for specific details about how these goals will be accomplished. Trinity prides itself on the talents of its faculty, and we fully expect them to rise to the challenges associated with providing engaging and student-centered instruction in a new format.

FAQs

Will the University will continue to accept the CBE given that the testing agencies have completely changed their exam and scoring methodologies?

We have received many questions regarding acceptance of AP, IB, and Cambridge A-Level examination credit due to changes in the administration and scoring of these exams. While there has been a significant impact surrounding the administration of such exams due to the global pandemic, Trinity will continue to accept qualifying scores without any changes to our existing equivalency rubric. All current policies pertaining to these exams will remain in place for the Fall 2020 entering class. Students are responsible for ensuring the scores are sent to us by the appropriate testing agency. Please feel free to contact the Office of the Registrar at registrar@trinity.edu should you have any questions about Trinity external credit policies.

Will the University allow students to shift the registration status for current courses to Pass/Fail?

In recognition of the extraordinary circumstances facing students as the University moved to remote synchronous learning, the office for Academic Affairs will accept and approve student requests to convert spring 2020 classes to pass/fail registration through the end of the fall 2020 semester (December 17, 2020). Effective immediately and through December 17, students may choose to switch all or some of their spring 2020 courses to pass/fail, including courses taken in their major, minor, or Pathways.

Students should continue to seek guidance from their advisers in making decisions regarding grading options relative to their individual circumstances and goals.

You may submit any new requests to Dr. Michael Soto, Associate Vice President for Academic Affairs, at msoto@trinity.edu until December 17, 2020.

Will students be able to use online courses from other colleges and universities as transfer credit this summer?

Trinity typically does not accept online courses for transfer credit, but this summer is obviously a special case. If a student requests the use of an online course from an accredited institution for summer 2020, Associate Vice President Michael Soto will encourage the relevant department chair to review the course materials carefully to determine whether the course fulfills the appropriate learning outcomes for Trinity equivalent credit. Upon the chairâ€™s recommendation, transfer credit from an online course for summer 2020 will be approved.

Will remote learning affect my credit hours in any way?

Trinity remains committed to supporting students' academic learning and allowing them to make timely progress toward graduation. All efforts will be made to enable students to complete their registered course hours, provided that students continue to satisfy course requirements.

What sort of hours should I expect my student to devote to synchronous remote learning?

As your student returns home, it is important to remember that they remain full-time students. As full-time students, they will be expected to attend scheduled course meetings at the appointed time (keep in mind that this may require time zone conversions). Attending virtual course meetings may require the use of the internet and/or telephone; when the internet is not available at home, your student may need to go to a place with wifi (e.g., local library). In addition to attending classes, full-time students have a variety of other responsibilities to be successful. This could include reading, completing homework assignments, meeting virtually with peers to complete group assignments, meeting with their professor or advisor during virtual office hours, writing papers, etc. At Trinity, we tell students that for every one credit hour, they can expect to spend at least three hours of effort per week inside and outside of class. For a three-hour course, this would amount to nine hours of effort per week on the activities named above. Students have great flexibility in when and how they complete their out-of-course activities. In order to enable your student to be successful this term, please keep this in mind, especially if you ask your student to take on additional responsibilities at home, including child care.

How will this affect summer session courses?

Trinity will deliver all summer courses using remote instruction. All courses will continue to fulfill whatever graduation or Pathways requirements they have been approved to fulfill, regardless of the format of delivery.

The Summer Course Schedule has already been released from the Registrarâ€™s Office. The pre-registration process and timeline is unchanged. Note, since the course schedule was developed in advance, it includes a classroom assignment, but this should be disregarded as all classes will be delivered remotely.

How will this affect study abroad in the summer of 2020?

Given the scope and scale of the COVID-19 pandemic and following the recommendation of the Center for International Engagement, Trinity has canceled all faculty-led study abroad programs for Summer 2020. We have determined that it is simply unsafe, and in many cases unfeasible, to offer these programs.

How will this affect study abroad in the fall of 2020?

In consultation with the Center for International Engagement, Trinity has made the difficult decision to cancel the faculty-led study abroad programs to Madrid and South Africa for Fall 2020. In addition, students will not be permitted to study abroad on programs led by other universities or third-party providers. Please know that the University did not make this decision lightly. We believe that preemptively canceling these programs now prioritizes the health and safety of our students and faculty while also giving students adequate time to prepare alternative arrangements, including the ability to register for courses for Fall 2020.

How will this impact undergraduate research and internships?

Because Trinity cannot offer appropriate medical care or quarantine for students who might become seriously ill, Trinity will suspend all â€œin-residenceâ€ aspects of our summer research and internship programs. On-campus housing will not be available to students who are involved in these programs. Students who are able to complete these activities remotely will continue to receive their stipends and retain the option of earning academic credit.

How will this affect the Universityâ€™s end-of-summer symposium?

We have chosen to cancel the symposium that typically marks the conclusion of the summer research and internship program. For students who complete sponsored research projects and sponsored internships this summer, we hope to schedule other presentation opportunities in the Fall semester. We know that many students use the summer research and internship symposium to fulfill the Oral and Visual Communication (OVC) requirement in the Pathways curriculum, and we will do everything possible to provide a comparable OVC option in the Fall.

What should I do if Iâ€™m planning to conduct research this summer?

Students and their faculty mentors should discuss their research plans as soon as possible. We encourage faculty mentors and students to continue their planned research partnerships using remote means during the established research period [5/18/2020 â€“ 7/30/2020] whenever possible.

What if my research canâ€™t be done remotely?

If the faculty mentor determines that the planned research cannot be delivered remotely, then we encourage students, faculty, and campus leaders to work together to try and find an alternate mentor to supervise the student. In the case that the research cannot be done remotely and no alternative can be found, the studentâ€™s involvement in the project will be cancelled. Faculty mentors have until Friday, May 1, to make the final decision as to whether or not their summer research plans will continue for the summer. We encourage faculty to make this decision as quickly as possible to allow students time to make alternate plans.

Can I travel abroad to conduct my research or internship?

Due to the current travel ban, University-sponsored travel is not permitted for students or faculty at this time.

How will this impact internships, research, and the Universityâ€™s Stumberg Competition?

The University will permit students to participate in remote internships or activities related to the Stumberg Competition, whether in San Antonio or in another location, to the extent that this is feasible and possible. We recognize that some employers and organizations may be positioned to provide remote internships while others may not. Recognizing that this situation continues to evolve and change, personnel in the Center for Experiential Learning and Career Success (CELCS) will share updated information regarding whether or not students have legal clearance to complete any portion of their internship on-site.

Is the Career Services office still open?

Career Services remains open and available to assist students remotely as they continue to pursue internships and job opportunities.

I live in San Antonio. Can I use resources on campus?

At this time, we cannot imagine that physical campus resources will be available to students over the summer, but online and remote resources are available to students at any time. For those participating in summer programs remotely, CELCS will provide additional resources about virtual mentoring and other resources to support the program. Additionally, Academic Affairs and other programs will offer resources and supplies to enable remote participation for both students and faculty.
