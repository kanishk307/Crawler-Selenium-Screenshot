Campus Spaces

Classrooms and Other Public Spaces

We are using the following principles to determine classroom use and density. These principles also apply to other public spaces or larger workspaces:

Given the kinds of interaction that occur in teaching, classes will only be held in rooms or spaces that have mechanical ventilation equipped with a recommended filtration system.

  • Note: Mechanical ventilation refers to the ability to bring fresh air into the space through the ventilation system. Good mechanical ventilation may be unrelated to good temperature control.
  • We have used a minimum of 6 feet of distance between occupants to determine classroom capacity or the capacity for occupancy of other spaces.
  • We will provide a few tents and more outside furniture for outdoor classrooms and small outdoor meeting/activities spaces. These spaces will need to be reserved.
  • We will not permit in-person shopping for classes during “shopping week.”

For offices or smaller workspaces, we will use the following practices:

  • If an office does not have mechanical ventilation, it should only be occupied by one person. Facilities will provide information to individuals about the ventilation of their office space for those who are interested. Reminder: good temperature control is not an indicator of good ventilation.
  • For spaces that do have good mechanical ventilation, we will maintain a separation of a minimum of 6 feet spacing or barriers between work spaces, or will work in shifts so that the space will be occupied by a smaller number of people to maintain distance.
  • For office hours or visitors to office spaces, we strongly encourage virtual meetings where possible. Necessary in-office meetings between individuals should only be held in spaces that have mechanical ventilation and by appointment, to eliminate congregating either in the office or in the hallways. We are seeking alternative meeting spaces for those community members who do not have mechanical ventilation in their office spaces or conference rooms.
  • While the experts we have consulted have all said that surfaces are a significantly less likely means of transmitting the virus, we will continue more frequent and more intensive cleaning of high-touch surfaces.

Currently, our health experts define a close contact as interaction at a distance of closer than 6 feet, for greater than 15 minutes with no masks. We are getting additional guidance from our medical experts, particularly about “talking intensive” activities that characterize a college campus, but this is helpful guidance as we think about some of our most likely activities. For example, this would suggest that if a student or faculty member tested positive, other people in their class would not be considered close contacts (if they abide by our behavioral measures).

While we will strive to maintain greater than 6 feet of separation in a laboratory, a faculty or staff member could help with a piece of equipment at a closer distance, as both parties would be masked and the interaction would be presumably shorter than 15 minutes. We are taking steps to manage traffic flow, but if we have spaces or moments where we need to pass more closely, this would not be considered as putting someone at risk. Again — we will get more information and confirmation about these assumptions for our context, but it is useful to help us understand how to minimize our risk.
 
While we expect to provide students with the full in-person experience, we also expect that some staff will work remotely. This will decrease the density of people on campus and will provide fewer vectors for virus transmission.
 
According to the Department of Education guidelines, while Montgomery County was in the yellow phase, non-instructional gatherings were limited to under 25 people, even if they were outside. The county moved to the green phase on June 26, raising the technical gathering limit to 250 people.

Cleaning

Housekeeping has been trained and checked on proper cleaning techniques. We are following the CDC guidelines on proper techniques and cleaning products.
 
Cleaning will be focused on high-touch surfaces in shared spaces.
 
The community will need to participate in keeping surfaces clean. We will distribute materials and employees are encouraged to wipe down their own high touch surfaces (e.g., desks) in the middle and at the end of the day, or when others have been in the space.
 
We are developing a specific plan for shared spaces and equipment and for classrooms between meetings.

Community Engagement

We will permit visitors to campus to support mission-related functions, provided that the visitors agree to abide by our safety measures and that none of our safety principles are violated in terms of the size and nature of the gathering, etc. A group of faculty and staff are developing guidelines for possible visitors that also address issues of safe travel. Since each visitor represents a possible vector of virus transmission, we will ask the faculty or staff member issuing the invitation to evaluate the need for an in-person visitor thoughtfully. The faculty/staff events group will share ideas for creating more dynamic remote events.
 
We aim to eliminate all visits to the campus from the general public. This includes the outside members of the gym and attendees beyond the Bi-Co community in campus activities.
 
All admissions tours and information sessions will be suspended through the end of 2020 unless conditions materially change.
 
We will treat Haverford College as an extension of our campus. That said, exchange between the campuses will undoubtedly be more limited due to safety considerations for the Blue Bus. Walking and biking offer additional options for student travel between the colleges for course exchange.
