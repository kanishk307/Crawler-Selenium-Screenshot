Returning to Campus

A Westminster education prepares you for the world’s challenges.
We will meet this one together.

Westminster is preparing campus and is excited to welcome Griffins home for Fall Semester. Some things will be different and we will all need to make some adjustments. A few things will be improved (Shaw got a makeover!) and we’re working hard to preserve the enriching college experiences important to you.

Academics

Academic Calendar

The Fall Semester calendar was reorganized to reduce the risk of spreading coronavirus. Fall break is removed, and the semester will end earlier in December.

Learn More

Course Instruction

Fall Semester course instruction will engage students in a combination of face-to-face and technology-assisted instruction that emphasizes student learning, health and safety.

Learn More

Course Instruction

Faculty at Westminster College are committed to providing high-quality student experiences. They are putting considerable time and energy into preparing courses for Fall Semester. Most classes will have a significant amount of in-person classroom time coupled with the use of various technologies to support student learning and student safety. Faculty are working together to share great ideas for the use of technology to maximize student-student and student-faculty engagement, both inside and outside of classrooms. Any necessary modifications to the academic course schedule will be announced by July 31.

What to Expect in Class

  • All classrooms will be set up for social distancing.
  • Face coverings will be required in class and other indoor spaces. Students must provide their own face coverings.
  • Eating in class will not be allowed.

Check for Symptoms

College leadership has set guidelines for everyone, including contractors and visitors, who are planning to come to the Westminster College campus. Before coming to campus, everyone must check themselves for symptoms. If you are not sick and must come to campus for classes or other obligations, follow all the guidelines for being on campus.

Before coming to campus or leaving your residence hall, check yourself for COVID-19 symptoms:

  • Cough
  • Shortness of breath
  • Fever (temperature over 100.3º F without the use of fever-reducing medication)
  • Chills
  • Repeated shaking with chills
  • Muscle pain
  • Headache
  • Sore throat
  • New loss of taste or smell

Anyone attending protests should monitor themselves for symptoms and seek testing and isolate themselves if symptoms develop.

Do not come to campus if:

  • You're experiencing COVID-19 symptoms
  • You feel sick
  • You or anyone you have been in close contact with has been diagnosed with COVID-19
  • You or anyone you have been in close contact with has been placed in quarantine
  • You have been asked to self-isolate or quarantine by a medical professional or local public health official

The restriction on close contact does not apply to health professionals who have performed urgent care work with appropriate personal protective equipment.

Wash Your Hands

Steps for washing your hands thoroughly:

  1. Wet your hands.
  2. Lather your entire hands.
  3. Scrub between your fingers and under your fingernails.
  4. Wash your hands for at least 20 seconds.
  5. Rinse and dry your hands.

Avoid touching your eyes, nose, or mouth with unwashed hands.

Handwashing is especially important after using the restroom, before eating, and after blowing your nose, sneezing, or coughing.

Supervisors should make sure their office is stocked with facial tissue and hand sanitizer, and ensure that there is easy access to hand-washing facilities.

Wear a Face Covering

Face Covering Requirements

In light of the available data about how COVID-19 spreads, the CDC recommends that people wear a cloth face covering to cover their nose and mouth in a community setting, especially in situations where you may be near people, to protect people around you if you are infected, but do not have symptoms.

Salt Lake County issued a face mask requirement that's in effect through at least Aug. 20.

Face covering requirements are in place for all who are on the Westminster College campus and physically able to comply. All individuals should keep their face covering with them and ready to use at all times. If you have any concerns about individual behavior, submit them through the COVID-19 comments, questions, and incident reporting form. Check the area you're in for signs that outline face covering requirements.

Wear your face covering when you:

  • Cannot maintain social distance between yourself and others
  • Are around others
  • Are moving through indoor spaces
  • Are in another department or office area
  • Are receiving maintenance or IS services in residential suites or offices
  • Are using an indoor study or lounge space
  • Are requested to wear one by someone in your proximity
  • Are evacuating a building because of a fire alarm or other emergency
  • Are in Shaw's food service area
  • Are in a multi-stall restroom
  • Are in a hallway of a campus building

Face Coverings and Social Distance

You do not need to wear a face covering when you're in a room or office by yourself. However, you need to use a face covering if someone enters the room and you are unable to maintain social distance.

Face coverings do not protect the wearer from exposure within 6 feet of another individual. Staying at least 6 feet away is always a good idea, but face coverings are particularly important when social distance cannot be maintained.

When outdoors, you should wear your face covering if you could come within 6 feet of someone else (e.g., passing each other on the bridge or on a narrow sidewalk). Even if you think you can maintain distance, you should have your face covering with you at all times and be ready to use it if needed. If you’re engaged in physical activity, such as running, jogging, and bicycling, you should keep at least 10 feet between yourself and others.

Types of Face Coverings

A re-usable cloth face covering can be a scarf, gaiter, neck wrap, bandana, or sewn cloth face mask. Face coverings can be made from a T-shirt or other materials that you can typically find at home. Medical masks are also appropriate to use. Re-usable cloth face coverings should be washed between each day of use or when they become visibly dirty. Disposable face coverings cannot be effectively cleaned for re-use.

Getting a Free Mask

A Mask for Every Utahn is a government initiative to help Utahns who may not have a face mask and are returning to the workplace or going out in public. The mask initiative is for all Utahns, however, orders are particularly encouraged from those who:

  • Don't already have a face mask
  • Aren't able to make a face mask or get one from someone else
  • Aren't able to purchase a face mask

Cloth face coverings should not be placed on young children (younger than 2 years of age), anyone who has trouble breathing or is unconscious, incapacitated, or otherwise unable to remove the cover without assistance.

Communication While Wearing a Face Covering

Face coverings can sometimes make it difficult to communicate, especially for people who have disabilities that impact speech or hearing. To effectively communicate while protecting yourself and others from coronavirus, you can plan to communicate through a video or phone call, or through written communication like email, text, or chat. If you’re trying to communicate during an in-person interaction, you can write your conversation on a notepad using individual pens or type your conversation out on your phones. You may also want to consider using a clear face shield or face mask to make it easier to see facial expressions and read lips.

Social Distancing in the Workplace

Westminster offices should increase the physical distance between employees in the workplace, which may require re-arranging workspaces temporarily.

Decrease social contact in the workplace by:

  • Limiting in-person meetings by conducting meetings over the phone, video, or chat.
  • Maintaining distance in break rooms or staggering lunch breaks to reduce the number of people using the space at one time.
  • Conducting necessary in-person meetings in a larger space which accommodates about 6 feet of distance between all attendees. If weather permits, meetings may also be held outdoors.
  • Moving toward an appointment-scheduling process to better control the flow of people in and out of offices that typically accommodate walk-in service requests and/or appointments.
  • Posting public health advisory flyers at office entrances and inside offices, reminding people of the importance of maintaining personal space.
  • Posting signs indicating that offices/workplaces/buildings are open only to members of the Westminster College community and not to visitors.

Westminster staff and student employees have remote work, paid leave, and unpaid leave options when working on campus is not an option.

Stay Home When Sick

If you're sick, stay home and avoid close contact with others. Call your health provider before arriving for medical care.

  • Avoid contact with people who are sick.
  • Avoid touching your eyes, nose, and mouth with unwashed hands.
  • Cover your mouth and nose with a tissue or sleeve when coughing and sneezing.

If you're sick or experiencing COVID-19 symptoms, follow these CDC guidelines:

  • Flu: Stay Home When You Are Sick

When to Safely be Around Others After COVID-19 Exposure, Symptoms, or Diagnosis

COVID-19 Isolation and Quarantine

If you were diagnosed with or likely had COVID-19, follow these CDC guidelines:

  • Discontinuation of Home Isolation for Persons with COVID-19
  • If you think or know you had COVID-19 and had symptoms, you can be around other people after you have had 3 days with no fever, symptoms have improved, and it’s been 10 days since symptoms first appeared.
  • If you tested positive for COVID-19, but had no symptoms, you can be around other people after 10 days have passed since your test. Depending on your health care provider’s advice and availability of testing, you might be tested to see if you still have COVID-19. If you will be tested again, you can be around others after you receive 2 negative test results in a row, at least 24 hours apart.
  • If you are immunocompromised due to medication or a health condition, you may need to self-isolate for longer than 10 days. A health care provider may recommend testing, which means you can be around others after you receive 2 negative tests in a row, at least 24 hours apart.
  • If you have had close contact someone with COVID-19, you should stay home for 14 days after exposure, which is based on the time it takes to develop illness.

Residential students who test positive for COVID-19 will be relocated during periods of quarantine.

Returning to Work

When it is safe for you to return to work, you must follow all the guidelines for being on campus.

Employees with Symptoms of Respiratory Illness

Employees must follow any individual guidance given to them by their physician and/or public health officials. You should be in communication with your supervisor regarding your status and must not return to work if you are continuing to experience any symptoms.

Employees with COVID-19

People with COVID-19 who have stayed home (home isolated) can stop home isolation under the following conditions.

If you will not have a test to determine if you are still contagious, you can leave home after these 3 things have happened:

  • You have had no fever for at least 3 full days without the use of medicine that reduces fevers
  • Other symptoms have improved (for example, when your cough or shortness of breath have improved)
  • At least 7 days have passed since your symptoms first appeared

If you will be tested to determine if you are still contagious, you can leave home after these 3 things have happened:

  • You no longer have a fever without the use of medicine that reduces fevers
  • Other symptoms have improved (for example, when your cough or shortness of breath have improved)
  • You received 2 negative tests in a row, 24 hours apart (your doctor will follow CDC guidelines)

Individuals with laboratory-confirmed COVID-19 who have not had any symptoms may discontinue home isolation when at least 7 days have passed since the date of their first positive COVID-19 diagnostic test and have had no subsequent illness.

Employees with the Flu

Employees should follow any individual guidance given to them by their physician, and stay in communication with their supervisor. In general, employees should stay home if they are sick until at least 24 hours after their fever (temperature of 100 degrees Fahrenheit or 37.8 degrees Celsius or higher) is gone. Temperature should be measured without the use of fever-reducing medicines (medicines that contain ibuprofen or acetaminophen).

Not everyone with flu will have a fever. Individuals with suspected or confirmed flu who do not have a fever should stay home from work at least 4–5 days after the onset of symptoms. Persons with the flu are most contagious during the first 3 days of their illness.

Employees with Other Illnesses

Employees should follow any individual guidance given to them by their physician, and stay in communication with their supervisor.

Keep Our Campus Clean

Regularly clean and disinfect shared work areas, including desks, tables, phones, computers, keyboards, and computer mice. Cleaning supplies will be distributed for use in classrooms and public-facing offices. A centralized ordering system for bottle refills and other supplies is being implemented for areas that need frequent re-supply of materials. Classroom cleaning kits will continue to be available for faculty who want to participate in more frequent cleaning. People using specialized equipment should disinfect the equipment they handle before and after use. More extensive protocols are in place for higher risk activities, such as using the locker rooms in HWAC.

To slow the spread of the coronavirus, all campus cleaning personnel were trained on the CDC recommendations for schools and are taking these extra precautions while cleaning campus facilities:

  • Cleaning personnel are carrying a spray bottle of disinfectant with them and continuously disinfecting doorknobs, restrooms, and commonly touched surfaces. Campus community members should be aware that the disinfectant in use can leave a slight residue on surfaces such as desks and door handles. Please know that disinfectant residue is normal.
  • Cleaning team supervisors are conducting more quality control checks and assisting cleaning personnel with following the new standards.
  • Two additional cleaning personnel are on campus deep cleaning shared spaces, disinfecting classrooms, and cleaning other areas not part of the normal routine.
  • The college's supply of disinfectant is being ordered and replenished regularly.
  • Public and common areas are being disinfected regularly.
  • Because many faculty and staff are working remotely, Westminster is suspending regular cleaning and the disinfecting of unoccupied offices through the end of summer. If you are going to be in your office and would like it to be cleaned, you can email Holly Patterson at hpatterson@westminstercollege.edu with your building and office number to make arrangements for cleaning and disinfecting to continue.

Avoid Behaviors of Bias

Be aware that concern around this evolving issue can lead to stigma and bias.

  • Coronavirus doesn’t recognize race, nationality, or ethnicity.
  • Wearing a face mask does not mean that a person is ill.
  • Submit a Bias Report if you see, hear, or read misinformation or harassment.
  • Show compassion and support for those who have been most closely impacted.

Residence Life

The Office of Residence Life has reduced the density of the residential halls and increased cleaning of all common spaces. Traditional style double-occupancy rooms are being converted to single-occupancy rooms. There will be a strict no-guest policy enforced during the 2020–21 academic year.

Move-in will be staggered to minimize potential exposure to and spread of COVID-19. Students will be assigned a move-in date and time and will be limited to having only two move-in helpers during their assigned time. The move-in process and procedures will be shared with students via their Westminster email by July 17.

All courses will be completed remotely after the Thanksgiving break, but students in residence halls can remain on campus during and beyond Thanksgiving, until the end of the term, with the expectation that they stay in Utah while on break.
